"Accessible and Useful": A Personal Mission


An interview with Software Engineer T.V. Raman, Google's expert on Web accessibility




Recently we talked with Google's crusader for Web Accessibility, Mountain View-based research scientist T.V. Raman, who spoke about his many accomplishments in the field of accessibility, his recent tour of Europe, and how he learned to "speak the information."

Could tell us a little about the work you do in Web Accessibility and Accessible Search?

T.V. Raman: My overarching goal is to make the Web--specifically, both Web content and Web applications--usable by everyone. "Everyone" is best explained to Googlers in terms of the "long tail." Our consumer-facing Web solutions do exceedingly well at addressing the needs of mainstream users with perfect hand-eye coordination and 20/20 vision. Now, how can we extend our reach to meet the needs for everyone from a senior citizen with deteriorating vision to a high-powered jet-setter confronting the Web with a small display?

Accessible Search was a project I created in 2006; it classifies Web pages by measuring visual complexity and then adds that signal into our scoring algorithm to provide a result set that is tweaked to promote high-quality pages that are also easier to listen to and navigate via a keyboard. The most satisfying bit about doing that project was that six months of work did more to raise awareness about creating accessible Web content than my ten years of evangelism about Web Accessibility. We have Google's reach to thank for that, and that in a nutshell is what makes working at Google so motivating.

What are some of the biggest challenges facing Web Accessibility?

TVR: How well Web content and applications work for a given individual at a given time is a function of:

1. How the content/application has been created.
2. The "lens" being used to view the Web (the browser).
3. The gap between the end-user's abilities and the expectations of the designers of the content/browser combination, and the adaptive technology being used to overcome this impedance mismatch.

Thus, "accessibility" is not a single bit that can be "checked off." It is in fact the usability question, only recast in terms of users in the long tail. From this perspective, the biggest challenge with respect to Web Accessibility today comes from the impedance mismatch that results when "static documents and interactive applications collide."

To explain briefly, a lot of today's ideas about Web Accessibility come from work on making electronic documents easier to consume. (For background, see my own graduate work, among others', on leveraging document structure to skim and browse content effectively.)

All of the requirements for making documents more readily usable by users who cannot see apply to the space of Web Applications. However, that in itself is not sufficient, since when you encounter something like Gmail or Google Reader you're no longer looking at a static document like the Declaration of Independence--these are now fully interactive applications. The biggest challenge on the Web today is therefore to take Web Accessibility to the next level, where we focus on web applications that are just as pleasurable to use for users in the long tail.

How did you come to Google?

TVR: After doing a PhD at Cornell where I built AsTeR (Audio System for Technical Readings), I worked at DEC Research, Adobe, and IBM Research before coming to Google. The insights I picked up from these work experiences convinced me that the next biggest opportunity was the move to Web Applications--something that made coming to Google a no-brainer.

AsTeR taught me that it was important to "speak the information" (rather than its visual representation). To see this, bring up an email message and compare how you speak it versus how you see it. At DEC, I transfered AsTeR's insights from the world of electronic documents to that of user interfaces; the result was Emacspeak.

I went back to document structure for the next few years at Adobe to move PDF from being a "look but don't touch" document format to one that was capable of holding document structure. Though the motivation was wider accessibility, it is also a good example of accessibility positively impacting the mainstream--document structure in PDF when implemented correctly helps extracting information from PDF documents, which benefits everyone.

I next spent 6 years at IBM Research with a focus on multi-modal interaction for the Web. One of the biggest stumbling blocks in building good spoken interaction for Web applications is the lack of higher-level structure and semantics in a typical HTML page. Addressing this problem led to the work on XForms--it enabled the authoring of Web Apps using a Model-View-Controller abstraction. Googlers can think of Google Mashup Editor, which is closest in spirit in terms of its underlying design.

So what's next?

TVR: I view Web Applications as the next big opportunity for making things more available and usable for users in the long tail. One insight from building an environment like Emacspeak is that to enable maximal flexibility with respect to meeting the end-users needs at the UI level, it is useful to separate the "head" of an application from its "body."

This is hard to do in the world of monolithic desktop software reminiscent of the PC era. But notice that when applications move to the Web, we necessarily separate the head from its body: the body of the application lives in the Web cloud (and consists of data and application logic), while the "head" is delivered to the Web Browser in the form of presentation and interaction logic. This leads to light-weight Web APIs, custom Web clients, and an incredible array of opportunities for opening up the choices available for users in the long tail.

What's your favorite thing about working at Google?

TVR: Google is one of the most intellectually stimulating places I've worked at--I usually describe it as graduate school with good food and money. More importantly, we live and breathe Web technologies at Google, and the incredible talent pool that we have assembled means that the world's expert on any given Web technology is somewhere down the hallway, figuratively speaking. Combine this with our culture of experimenting early without worrying about failure, and you get an incredibly stimulating work environment that also leads to rapid innovation.

What are some of your other research interests? Any pet passions or 20% projects?

TVR: Google Web APIs are one of my pet 20% projects, and I've been building a suite of clients for Google Services as a 20% project. The project is emacs-g-client, and its goal is to let users access all of Google's various services from within the comfort of Emacs (i.e., Web Applications are great, but they are more fun when one isn't limited by the vagaries of Firefox or IE).

Another project that I am very excited by is AxsJAX, a project I started with my officemate Charles Chen last fall after he started at Google.

Can you share some highlights from your recent trip to Europe to meet with media outlets, government officials, and NGOs?

TVR: The trip to the UK was a lot of fun. It was also very encouraging to see the level of interest in Accessibility in general, and our own ability to positively impact this space in particular. And being able to visit the Houses of Parliament and Number 10 (Downing Street) were personal highlights.

I'd like to thank Jessica Powell, Amy Fisher, Caroline Stanghon, and Julian Harty among others in the London office for both putting together an incredibly productive week while making me feel at home!

There's a remarkable YouTube video of you solving a Braille Rubik's cube in 24 seconds. What's your secret?

TVR: I solved the Rubik's cube after my brother put Braille dots on it for me. The first time took 4 days, but I eventually got very good at it and could solve it on average in under 30 seconds. I love puzzles in general, and my favorite mathematical toy (aside from the 5X5 Rubik's cube) is Zome System. You can see some pictures on my Picasa Album here.

(I took many of those pictures myself--and *all of them* were uploaded to Picasa from within Emacs using g-client.)

Anything else you'd like to share?

TVR: Here is a "call to arms" to all of Google Engineering:

The move to Web Applications in the cloud presents enormous opportunities with respect to how we work and play. We do an outstanding job with respect to handling the long tail of Web content in search; now let's innovate together to do the same at the user interaction level for users in the long tail--if we succeed we'll change the world even more dramatically than we already have!

Thanks very much!
